# Metabolism-Friendly Spices for Fish

Spices commonly associated with metabolic support that pair naturally with grilled fish. Note: effects in studies are modest — these are flavor-first upgrades with a possible bonus, not magic. Add to the garlic finish, the brine, or a dry rub.

## Best fits for merluza a la plancha

| Spice | How to use it here | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Cayenne / guindilla** (capsaicin) | Already in the bilbaína refrito and ajitos fritos | Capsaicin is the most-studied "thermogenic" spice; also just tastes right on fish |
| **Pimentón (smoked/sweet paprika)** | Ajada finish; dry rub with salt | Contains capsaicin-family compounds; core Spanish flavor |
| **Cumin** | Already in mojo verde; dry rub | Linked in some studies to improved lipid metabolism |
| **Black pepper** (piperine) | Crack over after the finish | Piperine studied for nutrient absorption + mild thermogenesis |
| **Ginger** | Grated into the garlic oil or mojo | Anti-inflammatory, mild thermogenic literature; pairs well with lime |
| **Turmeric + black pepper** | Small pinch in a dry rub (color!) | Curcumin absorption depends on piperine + fat (the olive oil helps) |
| **Cinnamon** | Not for fish — keep for oats/coffee in the high-protein section | Glycemic-control literature |

## A metabolism-forward dry rub for the plancha

Per 2 fillets, mix and dust on the dried fish seconds before searing:

- 1 tsp sweet pimentón
- ½ tsp cumin
- ¼ tsp cayenne
- ½ tsp black pepper
- (salt is handled by the brine — if you skip the brine, add ¾ tsp salt)

## Honest framing

The actual metabolic drivers: protein itself (fish is ~18–20 g protein/100 g with a high thermic effect), the olive oil's satiety, and overall diet. Spices are a small tailwind — use them because they make the fish taste like a restaurant made it.
