# Science: Frozen Fish, Water, and Browning

Why frozen merluza steams instead of sears — and the physics of fixing it.

## Why frozen fish gets watery

- Fish muscle is ~75–80% water. **Slow freezing** grows large ice crystals that shred cell membranes; on thawing the water can't be reabsorbed → drip loss = the puddle around thawed fillets. Fast (blast/IQF) freezing makes micro-crystals and far less damage.
- Temperature fluctuations in storage cause **recrystallisation** — small crystals merge into big ones. A bag full of frost means the fish has been temperature-abused.
- Lean white fish (hake is a gadiform, like cod) suffers extra: formaldehyde formed from TMAO during frozen storage cross-links proteins → spongy, watery texture. Lean species degrade faster than fatty ones.

## The hidden culprit: phosphates (E451 / STPP)

- Much commercial frozen white fish is soaked in **sodium tripolyphosphate** before freezing. It makes the muscle bind extra water — you pay for water, and that water floods your pan.
- **EU labelling protects you**: added water must appear in the ingredients; >5% added water must be in the product name; glazed products must state net weight *excluding* ice glaze.
- Buy: ingredients = "merluza" only, IQF, thin even glaze, no frost inside the bag.

## Maillard physics (why wet fish can't brown)

- Browning needs a surface temperature above **~140 °C**. As long as liquid water is present, the surface is capped at 100 °C. Every drop must boil off first — a wet fillet on a merely-hot plancha spends its whole cooking time steaming.
- Therefore: bone-dry surface + properly preheated plancha + no crowding (crowding traps steam and drops surface temp).

## Salt timing — the two valid windows and the dead zone

- **Seconds before cooking**: fine — no time to draw water out.
- **4–8 h dry-brine (uncovered, on a rack, in the fridge)**: salt pulls water out, then it's reabsorbed, firming the protein; the fridge air dehydrates the surface → best browning + juiciness.
- **10% wet brine, 5–10 min, then dry**: even seasoning throughout, measurably better moisture retention (salt partially dissolves fibres into a water-retaining gel; also reduces the white albumin that leaks out). Joan Roca brines hake; his extreme version cures 48 h and confits at 60 °C.
- **Dead zone: 15–45 min** — osmotic water sits on the surface. Avoid.

## Temperature & texture

- USDA safety line: 63 °C. Chef quality target for lean flaky fish: **~50–55 °C core** (Kenji's trials on flaky white fish incl. hake: 54 °C = "very moist, tender, flaky"; 60 °C = edge of dry). Spanish plancha sources also say 55 °C.
- Pull at ~52–55 °C; carryover adds a few degrees during the 2–5 min rest.
- Above ~60 °C the proteins contract and physically squeeze water out; hake has ~1–2% fat so there's nothing to mask it → chalky, crumbly, dry.
- White gunk (albumin) on the surface = you're at the edge of overcooking.

## Oils

- **Refined sunflower**: smoke point ~227–232 °C — technically fine for searing. High-oleic sunflower (common in Spain) is more heat-stable. But it's **neutral — adds no flavor**.
- **EVOO**: smoke point ~160–190 °C, but plancha cooking of thin fillets runs ~180–200 °C briefly — Spanish kitchens sear in EVOO anyway because the flavor is part of the dish. Refined olive oil (~240 °C) is the safe high-heat option.
- **Practice**: sunflower (or a blend) is fine on the plancha; the dish's flavor comes from the olive-oil garlic finish poured over at the end.

## Pro tricks worth keeping

1. Light flour dusting → golden crust, less sticking, less oil absorption.
2. Coarse salt scattered on the hot plancha, or oiled baking parchment under the fish → anti-stick insurance.
3. Fish spatula; temper fillets 20–30 min out of the fridge.
4. Don't move the fish — it self-releases when the crust forms.
5. Rest 3–4 min, crust-side up, sauce under or over — and serve immediately.
